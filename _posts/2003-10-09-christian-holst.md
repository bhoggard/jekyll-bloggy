<p>While some people have written <a href="http://www.popfactor.com/tmftml/archives/000818.html#000818">justifiably snarky</a> things about the <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/magazine/">Times Magazine</a>&#8217;s neo-70s issue, it has its moments, including the selection of artists in the photo essay, beginning with <a href="/2003/01/artsapoppin.html">Christian Holstad</a> as a 70s  roller disco kid.</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Christian Holstad, 31</strong></p>
<p>His art consists of knitting, quilting, collage, drawing, sculpture and, here in the foyer of Studio 54, himself, in full roller-disco regalia. In group and one-man shows this year, Holstad exhibited the following works: an installation of all the images he found filed under the label &#8217;&#8217;homosexual&#8217;&#8217; in the New York Public Library&#8217;s picture archives; photo collages based on White House interiors (one is of a faunlike nude boy peering out from behind a sofa in a reception room); and, at the Daniel Reich gallery, a plastic-enclosed bedroom, which was an homage to David Vetter, the &#8216;&#8217;bubble boy&#8217;&#8217; whose weakened immune system required him to live in a germ-free plastic bubble. Holstad, whose work will be included in the next Whitney Biennial, says:</p>
<p>&#8216;&#8217;When I came to New York City from Minnesota, I cried. I&#8217;d found my home, and I knew it. I got involved in the Williamsburg scene right away, and it was great.</p>
<p>There were these events where everyone would show up dressed in costumes; people would come with cardboard on their heads. And then it all died, and I left. Now I live in Greenpoint. Recently, I&#8217;ve gotten interested in the disco era, a time, like now, when New Yorkers were not afraid of darkness, and when the music was so inclusive everyone was dancing to it.&#8217;&#8217; </blockquote></p>
