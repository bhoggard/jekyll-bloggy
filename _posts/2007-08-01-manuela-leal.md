<p>Manuela Leal<br />
<em>Kalashnikov Close-Up</em>, 2007<br />
58 &#215; 78 Inches (4 parts)</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I haven&#8217;t seen this work in person. I saw it on <a href="http://manuelaleal.com/work/view/568/2809">Manuela Leal</a>&#8217;s <span class="caps">RSS</span> feed for her website.  Yes, I still actually follow via <span class="caps">RSS</span> every artist on <a href="http://www.artcat.com/">ArtCat</a>.  I have emailed with her about this series of work in the past, and learned a few things about where these come from.<br />
<blockquote><br />
These are color pencil and spray paint on inkjet prints. The images come from various war-related foreign videos on YouTube. I watch the videos and take stills from the computer screen with a digital camera. I then print the images, have layers of spray paint and then color pencils on top&#8230; I have been interested in war images and in &#8220;rebuilding&#8221; images of war and bombed, decimated buildings and places for a while now&#8230;</p>
<p>On YouTube, these videos are often posted as &#8220;responses&#8221; to other videos, as &#8220;justifications&#8221; (i.e.: a documentary on Croatian concentration camps will be posted in response to a serbian nationalistic video, and a video of a foreign mujahideen on the side of Bosnians torturing Serbian soldiers will be posted in response to a video on the massacre of Bosnians in Srebrenica by Serbs&#8230;).</p>
<p>I recognize that detached from language (they are often in languages I cannot understand), these images of war (and often propaganda) are posted, watched and commented on by people to whom images still carry immense urgency and power &#8211; they <strong>need</strong> to be seen&#8230;</p>
<p>I would like to know if&quot; art&quot; could still envision another reality, if by &#8220;altering&#8221; images one can offer the possibility &#8211; even if fictional &#8211; of change&#8230;</blockquote></p>
