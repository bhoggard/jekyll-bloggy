<p>The latest Village Voice has a good <a href="http://www.villagevoice.com/issues/0314/pollack.php">essay</a> by Barbara Pollack on the history of protest art.  One of my favorite paragragraphs is:</p>
<blockquote>Across the Met in the Assyrian gallery, artists gathered on March 5, Moratorium Day, to stage a more contemporary version of anti-war art-making, a &#8220;Draw-In for Peace,&#8221; organized by <a href="http://www.geocities.com/aawnion/">Artists Against the War</a>, and focusing attention to the wealth of archaeological treasures in Iraq, as well as the human life, that could be destroyed by American bombing. &#8220;If you are a serious artist, you don&#8217;t want to make work that is thought of as agitprop,&#8221; says Joyce Kozloff, one of the event&#8217;s organizers, &#8220;but now I feel that what I want is to learn to do that and fast.&#8221; </blockquote>
