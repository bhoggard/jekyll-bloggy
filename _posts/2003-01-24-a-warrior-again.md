<p><a href="http://www.newsday.com/news/opinion/columnists/ny-nyhen243101485jan24,0,4600251.column?coll=ny%2Dopinion%2Dcolumnists">Ellis Henican</a> on <a href="http://www.hackworth.com/">Col. David Hackworth</a>:</p>
<blockquote> David Hackworth is one of the most celebrated soldiers in modern U.S. history. He joined the merchant marine at 14, the Army at 15, and he&#8217;s never looked back. He was the youngest U.S. captain in the Korean War, the youngest colonel in Vietnam. As a soldier and later a war correspondent, he&#8217;s been on a dozen battlefields, hot and cold. And he never became a Pentagon bureaucrat. Of all the medals that have been pinned to his uniform, it&#8217;s the Combat Infantryman&#8217;s Badge he&#8217;s proudest of.<br />
<br />
Now his country is tilting toward war again.<br />
<br />
&#8220;Having thought long and hard about war with Iraq,&#8221; Hackworth told me, measuring his words carefully, &#8220;I cannot find justification. I don&#8217;t see a threat. They are not Nazi Germany. This is not the Wehrmacht. In no way does the situation in Iraq affect my nation&#8217;s security. That is the bottom line of analyzing threats. &#8216;Does this country threaten my country&#8217;s security?&#8217; In this case, absolutely not.&#8221;<br />
<br />
The awesome risks of this war, he said, far outweigh the potential rewards.<br />
<br />
&#8220;Focus on protecting the American homeland, which is not adequately defended,&#8221; Hack said. &#8220;Nine-eleven proved that. All of the machinations that have gone on since then are more lip service and crowd-pleasing than real. Our borders are still wide open. Our ports are vulnerable, too. And there are plenty of sleeper cells &#8211; Middle Eastern terrorists living among us, waiting to do their thing.&#8221;<br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
 And finally, what about all the anti-American sentiment this war will generate? &#8220;One and a half billion Muslims, who don&#8217;t like us anyway. Now they&#8217;re gonna look and say, &#8216;Here come the crusaders again.&#8217;&#8221;<br />
<br />
From their ranks rise the terrorists of tomorrow.<br />
<br />
As he travels across the country, Hackworth told me, the vast majority of military veterans he meets see this war as a rotten idea.<br />
<br />
&#8220;They&#8217;ve been there,&#8221; he said. &#8220;They know war is not a blood sport, as cable news make it out to be. Cheney and Bush and Wolfowitz and Rumsfeld &#8211; they&#8217;ve never stood and faced the elephant. These are the people who gush for war.&#8221;<br />
<br />
But don&#8217;t expect the generals and the admirals to raise their own private doubts.<br />
<br />
&#8220;Through the long eight-year bloodbath of Vietnam, not one general sounded off and said, &#8216;Bad war, can&#8217;t win it, let&#8217;s get out.&#8217; They went along to get along. It&#8217;s true again. The top generals are head-shakers.&#8221;<br />
<br />
As for the public, just watch how quickly the pro-war sentiment will evaporate.<br />
<br />
&#8220;My parachute brigade was the first to go to Vietnam,&#8221; Hackworth recalled. &#8220;Eighty-five percent of Americans were saying, &#8216;Hey, hey, all the way with <span class="caps">LBJ</span>.&#8217; We were there a year, shipping body bags back home as fast as we could. Suddenly, the American public, which is so fickle, did a 180. &#8216;Hey, hey, <span class="caps">LBJ</span>, how many kids did you kill today?&#8217;&#8221;</blockquote>
