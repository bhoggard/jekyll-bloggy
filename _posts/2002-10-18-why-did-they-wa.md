<p>So, the Bush administration knew about North Korea&#8217;s admission concerning its nuclear program <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A43722-2002Oct17.html">one week before the vote on Iraq in Congress</a>.  Were they waiting because they were afraid that Congress would raise questions about the logic of bombing Iraq but negotiating with North Korea?</p>
<p>Good <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2002/10/18/opinion/L18KORE.html">letter</a> in the NY Times today:</p>
<blockquote> In &#8220;North Korea Says It Has a Program on Nuclear Arms&#8221; (front page, Oct. 17), you say &#8220;the administration&#8217;s decision to keep news of the North Korean admission secret for the past 12 days while it fashioned a response appears significant for several reasons.&#8221;<br />
<br />
One reason not directly addressed in the article is the timing of the admission in relation to the Iraq resolution that was debated in Congress.<br />
<br />
The administration withheld its announcement about the North Korean nuclear program until the day President Bush signed the resolution.<br />
<br />
If this announcement had been made just a few days earlier, it would have been yet another reason for members of Congress to question the wisdom of invading Iraq over its hypothetical nuclear program when a real one exists in a different hostile government.<br />
<br />
The administration&#8217;s decision to withhold this information was apparently another cynical ploy to force a vote in Congress about Iraq while withholding critical and relevant facts.  <br />
<br />
<span class="caps">JEREMY</span> E. <span class="caps">MEYER</span><br />
Haverford, Pa., Oct. 17, 2002</blockquote>
<p>Interesting fact:  When the Assistant Secretary of State James A. Kelly returned from his trip to North Korea on October 5, he <a href="http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A48930-2002Oct5.html">canceled</a> his planned press conference.</p>
<p>I can&#8217;t believe anyone trusts this administration at all.</p>
