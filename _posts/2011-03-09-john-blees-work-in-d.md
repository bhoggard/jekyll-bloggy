<p>Below is an essay by David Matlock on John&#8217;s work.</p>

<p><strong>John Blee in <span class="caps">DUMBO</span></strong></p>
<p>As the 100th anniversary of Kandinsky&#8217; s breakthrough approaches, it is fair to ask: what<br />
has been achieved? Are abstract paintings today repeating what has already been said&#8212;<br />
and with each repetition, fading in strength? Or do they have something new to say, both<br />
from a technical standpoint and in terms of meaning?</p>
<p>At the beginning, abstraction exploded. Kandinsky himself tried to consolidate a more<br />
controlled language and connoisseurs still argue about his degree of success. When the<br />
Abstract Expressionists adopted the language on a larger scale, canvases exploded again<br />
in shamanic frenzy. Success was hit or miss, all too dependent on possession.</p>
<p>John Blee&#8217; s first mature paintings, dating from the early 1970s, were also shamanic,<br />
painted on the floor, and dependent on force and a possessed dancing. In a career of 40<br />
years, the man has achieved total control over paint and, more importantly, now owns<br />
his meditative inscape. He owns the land that earlier painters had to burst into by force.<br />
His paintings are deliberate acts of self-intoxication. (It is worth noting, that although he<br />
came of age in the 1960s, he has always disdained the use of recreational drugs.) The<br />
Hindu and Buddhist art he experienced as a child and adolescent in India were formative;<br />
as was the medieval sculpture in the caves of Ajanta and Ellora; and the work of Indian<br />
modernists in the National Museum. Blee responds to Asian art as an insider&#8212;someone<br />
who was shaped by the culture before he received his American inheritance.</p>
<p>The paintings on display are easy to enjoy but difficult to understand. From a technical<br />
standpoint, the rendering of space is unique. There is nothing arbitrary or &quot; atmospheric&quot;<br />
about the backgrounds&#8212;they are architectonic&#8212;that is, they create a definite space in<br />
which &quot; painterly event&quot; unfolds. It is easy to take pleasure in the paint&#8212;casual admirers<br />
often remark, &quot; What a painterly painter! What a colorist!&quot; without suspecting the hidden<br />
narrative. I strongly suspect the hieratic &quot; Sphinx&quot; (2009) is one of Blee&#8217; s dogs, posing<br />
nobly on the grass&#8212;the humorous title a reference to the difficulty of knowing what the<br />
animal is really thinking. These paintings are truthful because they begin from within<br />
and encompass the outside world in an ecstatic veil of paint. Earlier abstract painters<br />
discovered a new continent; John Blee is traveling inland and is providing a faithful<br />
record of what he finds.</p>
<p>John Blee studied with Helen Frankenthaler, Robert Moskowitz, and Robert Motherwell.<br />
His paintings have been shown in Paris, Moscow, Boston, Washington DC, and New<br />
York City (including the Andre Emmerich Gallery). His work is in the Museum of<br />
Modern Art and the Los Angeles County Museum.<br />
He currently living in Washington DC.</p>
<p>- David Matlock</p>
