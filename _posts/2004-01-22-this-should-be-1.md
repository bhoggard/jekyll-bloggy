<p>In the <a href="http://www.boston.com/news/nation/articles/2004/01/22/infiltration_of_files_seen_as_extensive/">Boston Globe</a>, via <a href="http://atrios.blogspot.com/2004_01_18_atrios_archive.html#107478226235201118">Atrios</a>:</p>
<blockquote>Republican staff members of the US Senate Judiciary Commitee infiltrated opposition computer files for a year, monitoring secret strategy memos and periodically passing on copies to the media, Senate officials told The Globe.<br />
<br />
From the spring of 2002 until at least April 2003, members of the <span class="caps">GOP</span> committee staff exploited a computer glitch that allowed them to access restricted Democratic communications without a password. Trolling through hundreds of memos, they were able to read talking points and accounts of private meetings discussing which judicial nominees Democrats would fight &#8212; and with what tactics.<br />
<br />
The office of Senate Sergeant-at-Arms William Pickle has already launched an investigation into how excerpts from 15 Democratic memos showed up in the pages of the conservative-leaning newspapers and were posted to a website last November.<br />
<br />
With the help of forensic computer experts from General Dynamics and the US Secret Service, his office has interviewed about 120 people to date and seized more than half a dozen computers &#8212; including four Judiciary servers, one server from the office of Senate majority leader Bill Frist of Tennessee, and several desktop hard drives.<br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
Democrats now claim their private memos formed the basis for a February 2003 column by conservative pundit Robert Novak that revealed plans pushed by Senator Edward M. Kennedy, Democrat of Massachusetts, to filibuster certain judicial nominees. Novak is also at the center of an investigation into who leaked the identity of a <span class="caps">CIA</span> agent whose husband contradicted a Bush administration claim about Iraqi nuclear programs.<br />
<br />
Citing &#8220;internal Senate sources,&#8221; Novak&#8217;s column described closed-door Democratic meetings about how to handle nominees.<br />
<br />
&#8230;</blockquote>
<p>Note that Robert &#8220;outing <span class="caps">CIA</span> agents&#8221; Novak is involved too.</p>
