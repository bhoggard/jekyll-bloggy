<p>Jean Rohe was the speaker at the New School graduation before John McCain.  Many of the students were understandably outraged that the president of the school, Bob Kerrey &#8212; a <a href="http://www.counterpunch.org/valentine.html">war criminal</a> &#8212; invited McCain to speak at the graduation.  His right wing ideology has nothing to do with the <a href="http://www.newschool.edu/history.html">progressive history</a> of the New School.</p>
<p>She has a post about her experience, including the text of her speech, at <a href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/jean-rohe/why-i-spoke-up_b_21358.html">The Huffington Post</a>.</p>
<p>Some excerpts are below:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It&#8217;s been noted in several columns that anti-McCain sentiment coming from the left may actually help him to garner support from the conservatives by giving him the opportunity to paint us as extremist liberals, so we should all keep our mouths shut. I say we need some &#8220;extremist liberals&#8221; if we&#8217;re ever going to get our democracy back. Others have said that he&#8217;s a moderate at heart and that we should let him continue pandering to the religious right so he can get the vote. Once he gets into office he&#8217;ll show his true colors and be the centrist he always was. I don&#8217;t buy that. People who truly care about human beings don&#8217;t vote for an unjust war, among other things, simply as a political maneuver. Enough said.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>More importantly, I feel obligated to respond to one thing that McCain told the New York Times. &#8220;I feel sorry for people living in a dull world where they can&#8217;t listen to the views of others,&#8221; he said. This is just preposterous. Yes, McCain was undoubtedly shouted-out and heckled by people who were not politely absorbing his words so as to consider them fully from every angle. But what did he expect? We could&#8217;ve all printed out his speech and chanted it with him in chorus. Did he think that no one knew exactly what he was about to say? And it was precisely because we listen to the views of others, and because, as I said in my speech, we don&#8217;t fear them, that we as a school were able to mount such a thorough and intelligent opposition to his presence. Ignorant, closed-minded people would not have been able to do what we did. We chose to be in New York for our years of higher education for the very reason that we would be challenged to listen to opposing viewpoints each and every day and to deal with that challenge in a nonviolent manner. We&#8217;ve gotten very good at listening to the views of others and learning how to also make our views heard, even when we don&#8217;t have the power of national political office and the media on our side.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Here is the conclusion of her speech:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>What is interesting and bizarre about this whole situation is that Senator Mc Cain has stated that he will be giving the same speech at all three universities where he has been invited to speak recently, of which ours is the last; those being Jerry Falwell&#8217;s Liberty University, Columbia University, and finally here at the New School. For this reason I have unusual foresight concerning the themes of his address today. Based on the speech he gave at the other institutions, Senator Mc Cain will tell us today that dissent and disagreement are our &#8220;civic and moral obligation&#8221; in times of crisis. I consider this a time of crisis and I feel obligated to speak. Senator Mc Cain will also tell us about his cocky self-assuredness in his youth, which prevented him from hearing the ideas of others. In so doing, he will imply that those of us who are young are too naÃƒÂ¯ve to have valid opinions and open ears. I am young, and although I don&#8217;t profess to possess the wisdom that time affords us, I do know that preemptive war is dangerous and wrong, that George Bush&#8217;s agenda in Iraq is not worth the many lives lost. And I know that despite all the havoc that my country has wrought overseas in my name, Osama bin Laden still has not been found, nor have those weapons of mass destruction.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Finally, Senator Mc Cain will tell us that we, those of us who are Americans, &#8220;have nothing to fear from each other.&#8221; I agree strongly with this, but I take it one step further. We have nothing to fear from anyone on this living planet. Fear is the greatest impediment to the achievement of peace. We have nothing to fear from people who are different from us, from people who live in other countries, even from the people who run our government&#8212;and this we should have learned from our educations here. We can speak truth to power, we can allow our humanity always to come before our nationality, we can refuse to let fear invade our lives and to goad us on to destroy the lives of others. These words I speak do not reflect the arrogance of a young strong-headed woman, but belong to a line of great progressive thought, a history in which the founders of this institution play an important part. I speak today, even through my nervousness, out of a need to honor those voices that came before me, and I hope that we graduates can all strive to do the same.</p>
</blockquote>
