<p>Our Constitution and the <a href="http://caselaw.lp.findlaw.com/data/constitution/amendment04/">Fourth Amendment</a> don&#8217;t really mean anything if Congress can pass laws abrogating it and the courts don&#8217;t care.</p>
<p>I can&#8217;t even go out to dinner without worrying whether the restaurant will be raided?</p>
<p>Alternet has a <a href="http://www.alternet.org/story.html?StoryID=15770">story</a> by an American who was held for an hour and a half in a Times Square area restaurant when it was raided by the <span class="caps">NYPD</span>, <span class="caps">INS</span>, and Department of Homeland Security.  I will quote some of it, but you should go read the whole thing.</p>
<blockquote>That night, March 20th, my roommate Asher and I were on our way to see the Broadway show &#8220;Rent.&#8221; We had an hour to spare before curtain time so we stopped into an Indian restaurant just off of Times Square in the heart of midtown. I have omitted the name of the restaurant so as not to subject the owners to any further harassment or humiliation.<br />
<br />
We helped ourselves to the buffet and then sat down to begin eating our dinner. I was just about to tell Asher how I&#8217;d eaten there before and how delicious the vegetable curry was, but I never got a chance. All of a sudden, there was a terrible commotion and five <span class="caps">NYPD</span> in bulletproof vests stormed down the stairs. They had their guns drawn and were pointing them indiscriminately at the restaurant staff and at us. <br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
The police placed their fingers on the triggers of their guns and kicked open the kitchen doors. Shouts emanated from the kitchen and a few seconds later five Hispanic men were made to crawl out on their hands and knees, guns pointed at them. <br />
<br />
After patting us all down, the five officers seated us at two tables. As they continued to kick open doors to closets and bathrooms with their fingers glued to their triggers, no less than ten officers in suits emerged from the stairwell. Most of them sat in the back of the restaurant typing on their laptop computers. Two of them walked over to our table and identified themselves as officers of the <span class="caps">INS</span> and Homeland Security Department. <br />
<br />
I explained that we were just eating dinner and asked why we were being held. We were told by the <span class="caps">INS</span> agent that we would be released once they had confirmation that we had no outstanding warrants and our immigration status was OK&#8217;d. <br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
&#8220;You have no right to hold us,&#8221; Asher insisted.<br />
<br />
&#8220;Yes, we have every right,&#8221; responded one of the agents. &#8220;You are being held under the Patriot Act following suspicion under an internal Homeland Security investigation.&#8221; <br />
<br />
When I asked to speak to a lawyer, the <span class="caps">INS</span> official informed me that I do have the right to a lawyer but I would have to be brought down to the station and await security clearance before being granted one. When I asked how long that would take, he replied with a coy smile: &#8220;Maybe a day, maybe a week, maybe a month.&#8221; <br />
<br />
We insisted that we had every right to leave and were going to do so. One of the policemen walked over with his hand on his gun and taunted: &#8220;Go ahead and leave, just go ahead.&#8221; <br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
As I continued to press for legal counsel, a female officer who had been busy typing on her laptop in the front of the restaurant, walked over and put her finger in my face. &#8220;We are at war, we are at war and this is for your safety,&#8221; she exclaimed. As she walked away from the table, she continued to repeat it to herself? &#8220;We are at war, we are at war. How can they not understand this.&#8221; <br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
After an hour and a half the <span class="caps">INS</span> agent walked back over and handed Asher and me our licenses. A policeman took us by the arm and escorted us out of the building. Before stepping out to the street, the <span class="caps">INS</span> agent apologized. He explained, in a low voice, that they did not think the two of us were in the restaurant. Several of the other patrons, though of South Asian descent, were in fact U.S. citizens. There were four taxi drivers, two students, one newspaper salesman &#150; unwitting customers, just like Asher and me. I doubt, though, they received any apologies from the <span class="caps">INS</span> or the Department of Homeland Security. </blockquote>
