<p><img alt="artfairs-2006.jpg" src="/mt/archives/artfairs-2006.jpg" width="405" height="403" /></p>
<p>I was surprised by the number of people who had really negative things to say about the <a href="http://www.scope-art.com/fairs/">Scope fair</a>.  I found it to be a great deal of fun, and filled with a lot of interesting art.  I particularly enjoyed a number of the German galleries.  Check out the <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/bloggy/tags/nycscope2006/">photos I took at Scope</a>.  I suspect some people found the wackier stuff, including the installations in the middle, not &#8220;serious&#8221; enough and therefore wrote off the whole fair as unserious and lacking in good work.  The way <a href="http://fromthefloor.blogspot.com/2006/03/seen-and-scene-at-scope.html">Todd Gibson</a> described it makes sense to me and is closer to my view of it.</p>
<p>Many of the anti-Scope crowd also complained that <a href="http://www.thearmoryshow.com/">The Armory Show</a> was too much about money. I don&#8217;t see how we&#8217;re going to get some magical middle ground, in which the market rewards precisely the correct people, and no more.  I also find the attacks on Scope a bit humorless.  I&#8217;m big on the idea of having a huge variety of work available for us to see, knowing some of it will be good and some will be bad.  I like this part of Art Fag City&#8217;s <a href="http://artfagcity.blogspot.com/2006/03/armory-insert-yawn-here.html">post</a> on Armory:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>No one can be impervious to the sales hype the fair brings out, and since so much of what is shown at this fair pushes a formalist aesthetic, it is hard to know the difference between those things that are legitimately good and what merely looks good. Everything looks like you should buy it.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Lately I feel I have encountered a lot of art that feels middlebrow, but is made with great skill.</p>
<p>While I find some aspects of the current market off-putting, I&#8217;m also happy to see a huge number of artists making money from their art.  Under those conditions, froth happens.  Yes, a lot of people that make crappy art will be deemed marketable, but I prefer that outcome to one where almost no one sells work unless its already certified as blue chip.  We live in a country where there is very little public funding for art.  Even if there were, given the Neanderthals in charge, we would probably end up with a lot of flag paintings, and I don&#8217;t mean in the style of <a href="http://www.nga.gov/feature/artnation/johns/biography_2.shtm">Jasper Johns</a>.</p>
