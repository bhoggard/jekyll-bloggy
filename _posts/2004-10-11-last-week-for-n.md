<p>We saw The Civilians&#8217; <em>Nobody&#8217;s Lunch</em> last Thursday, and I&#8217;ve been remiss in not writing it about it sooner.</p>
<p>A play at the <a href="http://www.ps122.org/">place</a> where <a href="http://www.andyhorwitz.com/">Andy</a> works, recommended by both <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2004/10/10/arts/10guide.html">Choire</a> and me?  How could you miss it?  Besides, for the visual arts readers of this blog, the cast includes <a href="http://www.pinkghettoproductions.com/">Alix Lambert</a>.</p>
<p>I <a href="/2004/09/the-civilians-n-1.html">mentioned</a> it earlier, but that was before we had seen it.</p>
<p>I think it is still more of a work in progress than <i><a href="/2003/10/the-civilians-g.html">Gone Missing</a></i>, but that isn&#8217;t really a complaint for a work from a group like The Civilians.  Heck, I saw Wooster Group&#8217;s &#8220;To you, the birdie&#8221; three times in various stages of completion.</p>
<p>The theme of <em>Nobody&#8217;s Lunch</em> is epistemology &#8212; how do we know what we know?  The stories range from what we get when the cast cold-called every Jessica Lynch in a phone book anywhere in America, to the&#8230; spirit channeled by one of Damian Baldet&#8217;s characters who says alien creatures feed on our fear and love the world America has created.  In a strong cast, he really stands out with a brilliant performance in the piece.</p>
<p>There is plenty of humor in the work, but there are also moments that made me pretty emotional.  Christina Kirk&#8217;s character that tells us of her childhood experience in a cult is chilling.  KJ Sanchez gives us part of an interview with her 73-year-old mother, and when it&#8217;s followed by Baron Vaughn singing &#8220;I want to die for something&#8221;, I will admit James and I got a bit teary.</p>
<p>In addition to a brilliant cast, Michael Friedman&#8217;s songs really stand out.  I don&#8217;t have to tell anyone who knows me that I hate conventional musicals, but there are ones like Hedwig or Urinetown that I do like.  Generally, they are ones with clever music that sounds as if it&#8217;s aware of what&#8217;s happpened since 1940 in music, and that has some political content.  Michael&#8217;s lyrics are very smart, and when he pulls a line into a song that you heard a character speak earlier in the evening, it adds additional depth.  He writes in a number of styles, and one of my favorites in this one is the <em>Song of Progressive Disenchantment</em>, performed by Caitlin Miller.  As you might guess, it&#8217;s in the style of a Brecht/Weill song, such as <em>Surabaya Johnny</em>.  It&#8217;s also hilarious.</p>
