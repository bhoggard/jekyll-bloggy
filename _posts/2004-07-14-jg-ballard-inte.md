<p>Via <a href="http://rodcorp.typepad.com/rodcorp/2004/07/interviews_and_.html">rodcorp</a>, I found this <a href="http://books.guardian.co.uk/departments/sciencefiction/story/0,6000,1245664,00.html">interview</a> with J.G. Ballard in The Guardian.  Fascinating stuff, including his interest in the visual arts.  An excerpt:</p>
<blockquote>Today&#8217;s art scene? Very difficult to judge, since celebrity and the media presence of the artists are inextricably linked with their work. The great artists of the past century tended to become famous in the later stages of their careers, whereas today fame is built into the artists&#8217; work from the start, as in the cases of Emin and Hirst.<br />
<br />
There&#8217;s a logic today that places a greater value on celebrity the less it is accompanied by actual achievement. I don&#8217;t think it&#8217;s possible to touch people&#8217;s imagination today by aesthetic means. Emin&#8217;s bed, Hirst&#8217;s sheep, the Chapmans&#8217; defaced Goyas are psychological provocations, mental tests where the aesthetic elements are no more than a framing device.<br />
<br />
It&#8217;s interesting that this should be the case. I assume it is because our environment today, by and large a media landscape, is oversaturated by aestheticising elements (TV ads, packaging, design and presentation, styling and so on) but impoverished and numbed as far as its psychological depth is concerned.<br />
<br />
Artists (though sadly not writers) tend to move to where the battle is joined most fiercely. Everything in today&#8217;s world is stylised and packaged, and Emin and Hirst are trying to say, this is a bed, this is death, this is a body. They are trying to redefine the basic elements of reality, to recapture them from the ad men who have hijacked our world. </blockquote>
<p>I am currently reading Ballard&#8217;s <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0374525765/tristanmedial-20"><i>War Fever</i></a>, a rather prescient set of short stories published in 1999.</p>
