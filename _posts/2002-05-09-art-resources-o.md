<p>Last night we went to the opening for 3 shows at <a href="http://www.artretran.com">Art Resources Transfer</a> &#8212; Jonathan Feldschuh, <a href="http://www.artnet.com/ag/fineartthumbnails.asp?gid=135&cid=2829">Merrill Wagner</a>, and M. Sovan Kumar.  Jonathan also has a show at <a href="http://www.cynthiabroan.com/">Cynthia Broan</a> right now.  The pottery installation by Kuman is really beautiful, and I <span class="caps">LOVED</span> the Merill Wagner works.  She rocks, as usual.</p>
<p>After the opening we saw <u>Blue Surge</u>, by Rebecca Gilman, at the <a href="http://www.publictheater.org/">Public Theater</a>.  Info on the production in Chicago at the Goodman is available <a href="http://www.goodman-theatre.org/press/0615bluesurgepremiere.html">here</a> and <a href="http://centerstage.net/theatre/reviews/bluesurge.html">here</a>.  It seems to be mostly the same cast, and the same director as that production.  She has a reputation for writing plays that are a bit didactic, and have their hearts in the right place, but don&#8217;t necessarily work as theatre.  This is the first play of hers I&#8217;ve seen, and I have to agree.</p>
<p>I think the direction by Robert Falls, and the cast, outshined the play.  The cast was uniformly <b>excellent</b>.  Rachel Miner was totally convincing as a young woman still in her teens who realizes that working in the local &#8220;massage parlor&#8221; makes more sense for her than working at Wendy&#8217;s.  Joe Murphy plays a cop trying to &#8220;escape&#8221; his background and make something of himself, with Amy Landecker as his middle class artist girlfriend.  The other two actors, Steve Key as a goofy, slacker cop, and Colleen Werthmann (member of <a href="http://www.elevator.org/">Elevator Repair Service</a>) as another hooker, are also awesome.  We spoke to Key afterwards to tell him how much we enjoyed it.</p>
<p>The play has its moments, I will admit.  The scene between Beth (upwardly mobile) and her boyfriend Curt (who isn&#8217;t) is really strong.  All of us that came from small, poor towns where most people have dead end jobs and drink too much would find a lot that&#8217;s familiar in that scene.  He wonders if she stays with him to shock her artist friends, and she&#8217;s disappointed that he&#8217;s not more successful.  After all, she says, anyone who is smart enough and tries hard enough can &#8220;make it&#8221; in America.  I would like to think that, and I&#8217;m lucky to be where I am now compared to <a href="http://paragould.ar.totalwebgov.com/">where I came from</a>, but I don&#8217;t think that&#8217;s the way the world works for most people.  I went to a high school where most people didn&#8217;t think of going to college, and thought it was frivolous to do so.  I&#8217;m glad I escaped, but I can&#8217;t really say that I&#8217;m &#8220;better&#8221; than the people who are still there.  I&#8217;m lucky.</p>
