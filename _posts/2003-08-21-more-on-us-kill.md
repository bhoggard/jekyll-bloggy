<p>I posted something <a title="bloggy: Sergio Vieira De Mello, Mazen Dana" href="/2003/08/sergio-vieira-d.html">a couple of days ago</a> in which I said I was very disappointed to see so little coverge of the U.S. killing journalists like Mazen Dana in the blogosphere.  Yesterday, the Boston Globe had a pretty fierce editorial, <a href="http://www.boston.com/dailyglobe2/232/editorials/A_cameraman_killed+.shtml">A cameraman killed</a>, which I&#8217;ll excerpt (emphasis mine):</p>
<blockquote><span class="caps">WITH</span> <span class="caps">AMERICAN</span> soldiers being killed almost daily in Iraq, nervousness among the occupying forces is understandable. But there is no excuse for US troops gunning down a TV cameraman doing his job, as happened to a prize-winning Reuters newsman on Sunday.<br />
<br />
Despite the fact that Mazen Dana, 43, a father of four, had received permission from a US military official to film on the site, where other newsmen were also working, soldiers on two approaching tanks thought he might be an Iraqi guerrilla and his camera a rocket-propelled grenade launcher, according to reports. They shot first and attempted resuscitation later, unsuccessfully.<br />
<br />
Eighteen journalists have died in Iraq from hostile fire and accidents. Five have been killed by the US military.<br />
<br />
&#8230;<br />
<br />
It is only natural that such organizations &#8212; and newspaper editorials &#8212; react with outrage when colleagues are killed on the job. <b>But these killings raise a broader question of whether the coalition rules of engagement are too aggressive with all civilians. Reports of excessive force against Iraqis mount: US soldiers even killed two Iraqi policemen they mistook for criminals recently. Such actions inflame local passions, making it all the harder for occupying forces to keep the peace.</b></blockquote>
<p>There is also a Salon <a href="http://www.salon.com/news/feature/2003/08/20/photographer/">article</a>   &#8212; worth watching a free ad to read &#8212; on how some journalists feel they are being targeted deliberately.</p>
<blockquote> &#8220;From the eyewitness accounts, it appears that Dana was fired on without warning,&#8221; wrote the Committee to Protect Journalists in an open letter to Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld. &#8220;He was filming in an area where no hostilities were taking place, raising questions about whether U.S. troops acted recklessly in targeting him.&#8221;</blockquote>
