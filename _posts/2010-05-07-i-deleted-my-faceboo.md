<p>I don&#8217;t have a lot of illusions about privacy when using social media such as <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/bloggy/">Flickr</a> or <a href="http://twitter.com/bhoggard">Twitter</a>, but there is a difference when a company like Facebook behaves in a really sleazy fashion.</p>
<p>I work on websites every day, including my own such as the art calendar <a href="http://www.artcat.com/">ArtCat</a>.  I did not start out with one privacy policy for the calendar, and then gradually claim the right to use more and more information submitted to us.  For example, I could offer a list of contemporary art galleries for sale to advertisers or artists looking for representation, but that would be wrong because it&#8217;s not what the galleries expected when they gave information to us.  However, given the <a href="http://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2010/04/facebook-timeline">changes in Facebook&#8217;s privacy policy</a> since 2005, they would consider this perfectly reasonable behavior.</p>
<p>In addition, with recent changes to their development platform, <a href="http://www.readwriteweb.com/archives/how_to_delete_facebook_applications_and_why_you_should.php">Facebook applications have more and more access to your private data</a>, including applications you have not chosen to install, but your friends have. Want to share information only with friends?  You&#8217;re sharing it with applications that your friends use.</p>
<p>And how about those <a href="http://royal.pingdom.com/2010/04/29/facebook-as-a-single-point-of-failure-for-the-web/">neat new sharing tools</a> introduced by Facebook?  Until they corrected a bug, visiting sites that are using Open Graph allowed them to <a href="http://www.macworld.com/article/151087/2010/05/facebook_addingapps.html">install an application to your profile without asking you</a>.  Given their privacy track record, including the recent <a href="http://www.pcworld.com/businesscenter/article/195693/facebook_bug_exposes_private_chats.html">exposure of private chats</a>, I wouldn&#8217;t trust them to fix those holes quickly.  &#8220;Instant personalization&#8221; indeed.</p>
