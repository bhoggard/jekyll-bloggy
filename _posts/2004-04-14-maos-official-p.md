At ease: Hou Bo&#8217;s portrait of Mao and family at the seaside</p>
<blockquote>When Mao Zedong proclaimed his new Socialist China in October 1949 from the great gate of Tiananmen, he walked to the balcony&#8217;s edge, looked over to the cheering crowds, and called out: &#8220;Long live the people!&#8221; Moments later, he was captured by the photographer Hou Bo, in that now-famous image, as he declared into the microphone: &#8220;The Chinese people have stood up.&#8221;<br />
<br />
In the photograph, we don&#8217;t see the Chinese people themselves, listening in the square below. Mention of them &#8211; and even the greeting Mao had used &#8211; would soon become subversive. The next time Tiananmen Square would hear &#8220;Long live the people!&#8221; was 40 years later, when it was shouted by students calling for democracy, shortly before the tanks moved in.<br />
<br />
The photograph of Mao on the balcony can now be seen in a fascinating and disturbing exhibition at the Photographers&#8217; Gallery, London, that is largely devoted to the work of Hou Bo and Xu Xiaobing, the husband-and-wife team who became Mao&#8217;s official photographers. </blockquote>
- <a href="http://www.guardian.co.uk/arts/features/story/0,11710,1189143,00.html">Monster at the Beach</a>, The Guardian, April 10, 2004
