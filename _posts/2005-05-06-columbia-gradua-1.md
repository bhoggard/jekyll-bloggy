<p>A couple of weeks ago, I <a href="/2005/04/grad-students-o.html">wrote</a> about a strike by the graduate students at Yale, who do much of the teaching.  <em>The Nation</em> has a <a href="http://www.thenation.com/doc.mhtml?i=20050509&amp;s=washburn">web article</a> on what&#8217;s happening at Columbia by Jennifer Washburn, a fellow at the New America Foundation and the author of <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0465090516/tristanmedial-20">University, Inc.: The Corporate Corruption of Higher Education</a></em>.</p>
<p>Columbia is using tactics that would be illegal for any regular employer to use, but the private universities have seen to it that grad students aren&#8217;t covered, and have hired union-busting law firms to advise them.  <em>The Nation</em> has a <a href="http://www.thenation.com/special/pdf/brinkley_letter.pdf">pdf</a> of a letter from liberal historian Alan Brinkley, currently Columbia&#8217;s Provost, discussing retaliatory actions to be used against students trying to organize.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The memo, dated February 16, 2005, is signed by none other than Alan Brinkley, a well-known liberal historian who is now serving as Columbia&#8217;s provost. Brinkley has gone out of his way to assure outside observers, including New York State Senator David Paterson, that &#8220;students are free to join or advocate a union, and even to strike, without retribution.&#8221; Yet his February 16 memo, addressed to seventeen deans, professors and university leaders, lists retaliatory actions that might be taken against students &#8220;to discourage&#8221; them from striking. Several of these measures would likely rise to the level of illegality if graduate student employees were covered under the National Labor Relations Act.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Such measures include telling graduate student teachers and researchers who contemplate striking that they could &#8220;lose their eligibility for summer stipends&#8221; (i.e., future work opportunities) and also &#8220;lose their eligibility for special awards, such as the Whitings&#8221; (a prestigious scholarship and award program). Yet another proposal cited in the memo would require students who participated in the strike &#8220;to teach an extra semester or a year&#8221; as a condition for receiving their scholarly degree.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Not a very nice example for some of the wealthiest institutions in the country to be setting.</p>
