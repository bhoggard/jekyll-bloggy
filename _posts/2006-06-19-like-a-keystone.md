<p>I just read an <a href="http://www.newsday.com/news/opinion/ny-oplem194788259jun19,0,6463888.story?coll=ny-viewpoints-headlines">op ed</a> in <em>Newsday</em> by Ray Lemoine.  Ray LeMoine is co-author, with Jeff Neumann and Donovan Webster, of <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1594200912/tristanmedial-20"><em>Babylon by Bus</em></a>, an account of LeMoine and Neumann&#8217;s experiences working for the Coalition Provisional Authority in Iraq.</p>
<p>He points out that he had just spent six months working and traveling in the Islamic world &#8212; Turkey, Lebanon, Egypt and Pakistan &#8212; so that he wasn&#8217;t surprised when the Department of Homeland Security agents took him aside to question him.  What did they want to know about?  Copyright infringement related to t-shirt sales.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>No, these frontline warriors in the global war on terrorism at Homeland Security had far more pressing issues to question me about. &#8220;Why did you infringe on the Boston Celtics&#8217; copyright in Boston in 2003?&#8221; asked my case officer, Malik &#8211; ironically a Pakistani &#8211; from behind his high desk. Uh, because I used to sell T-shirts outside sporting events, I said, wondering what this had to do with national security.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>&#8220;You&#8217;ve got a long record,&#8221; he said. Sure, for peddling &#8220;Yankees Suck&#8221; T-shirts &#8211; sans permit, which isn&#8217;t a crime but a code violation &#8211; not for promoting &#8220;Bin Laden Rulz!&#8221; DVDs or the &#8220;Idiot&#8217;s Guide to Suicide Bombing.&#8221;</p>
</blockquote>
<p>They also had information on a dispute with a parking attendant in New York.  Apparently, the <span class="caps">NYPD</span> now feels the need to share basically all of everyone&#8217;s record of police contact with the <span class="caps">DHS</span>.  Do you think they can really process the amount of information they&#8217;re given?  Are the feds really in charge of policing all behavior now?</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Homeland Security, the $40-billion-a-year agency set up to combat terrorism after 9/11, has been given universal jurisdiction and can hold anyone on Earth for crimes unrelated to national security &#8211; even me for a court date I missed while I was in Iraq helping America deter terror &#8211; without asking what I had been doing in Pakistan among Islamic extremists the agency is designated to stop.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Instead, some of its actions are erasing the lines of jurisdiction between local police and the federal state, scarily bringing the words &#8220;police&#8221; and &#8220;state&#8221; closer together. As long as we allow Homeland Security to act like a Keystone Stasi, terrorism will continue to win in destroying our freedom.</p>
</blockquote>
