<p>I read <a href="http://www.theregus.com/content/4/25435.html">this</a> after installing the newest Microsoft &#8220;security update&#8221; unfortunately.  I&#8217;ve now agreed for Microsoft to connect to my machine and &#8220;fix&#8221; stuff whenever they feel like it.</p>
<blockquote>If you caught our <a href="http://theregister.co.uk/content/4/25942.html">recent coverage</a> of the Windows Media Player trio of security holes you may have followed a link to the TechNet download site for a patch, or you might have activated Windows Update. If you did the former (though, oddly, not if you did the latter), you would have been confronted with an End User License Agreement (<span class="caps">EULA</span>) stating, most ominously, that:<br />
<br />
&#8220;You agree that in order to protect the integrity of content and software protected by digital rights management (&#8216;Secure Content&#8217;), Microsoft may provide security related updates to the OS Components that will be automatically downloaded onto your computer. These security related updates may disable your ability to copy and/or play Secure Content and use other software on your computer. If we provide such a security update, we will use reasonable efforts to post notices on a web site explaining the update.&#8221; </blockquote>
<p>Not so comforting, coming from a company that <a href="http://theregister.co.uk/content/4/25738.html">accidentaly distributed virus-infected software</a> recently.</p>
