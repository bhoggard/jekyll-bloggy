<p>Via <a href="http://www.culturebot.org/archives/2006/02/09/UziwieldingHobosSpreadingGrief.php">Culturebot</a> I found this great <a href="http://www.timeout.com/newyork/Details.do?page=1&amp;xyurl=xyl://TONYWebArticles1/541/theater_broadway/hole_in_none.xml">slapdown</a> by David Cote in Time Out New York of mediocre, safe uptown theater.  More reviews should be like this.  <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/people/t/anthony_tommasini/">Anthony Tommasini</a> can be counted on to write similar, yet more polite, things about how bad classical music institutions are these days.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>David Lindsay-Abaire&#8217;s <em>Rabbit Hole</em> made me sick. During this competent dramedy about the mourning process, I experienced bizarre hallucinations, nausea, confusion and an irritability verging on dyspepsia. Upon learning my theater-going patterns, the doctor delivered a swift diagnosis of Biltmore Syndrome. It&#8217;s a fairly common condition brought about by seeing too many middlebrow, bourgeois plays at New York&#8217;s big nonprofit theaters. The disease gets its name, obviously, from MTC&#8217;s Biltmore Theatre, which has been home to a steady stream of unimaginative comedies and dramas about middle-class angst since it opened in 2003.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>&#8230;</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>No doubt there&#8217;s a thrilling play to be written about grief and recovery in suburbia, but this isn&#8217;t it. And until Lindsay-Abaire and his supporters at <span class="caps">MTC</span> crack the code, you&#8217;ll learn more from Oprah and Dr. Phil than from this pabulum. Personally, I&#8217;d rather see a ripping tale about Uzi-wielding hobos spreading grief around than brave homeowners suppressing it.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>But then, I&#8217;m not an <span class="caps">MTC</span> subscriber or ticket buyer; I don&#8217;t have to justify seeing money wasted on an expensive-looking set and the blandest stars that money can buy. John Lee Beatty&#8217;s lavish Westchester interiors, spinning on hydraulic turntables, stand as smug, imagination-murdering monuments to MTC&#8217;s wealth and, presumably, the well-appointed estates of some percentage of its subscribers. And, like its aesthetic clones, the Roundabout Theatre Company and Lincoln Center Theater, <span class="caps">MTC</span> attracts big names regardless of whether they possess real talent.</p>
</blockquote>
