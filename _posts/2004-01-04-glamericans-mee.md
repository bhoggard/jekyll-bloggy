<p>We went to a preview today of Big Art Group&#8217;s <a href="http://www.bigartgroup.com/honomo.htm">House of No More</a> at <a href="http://www.ps122.org/artists/show.php?showID=397">P.S.122</a>.  Disclaimer: I got comp tickets from Andy at P.S.122, as someone who attends a lot of shows there.  It was sort of a friends/press/tastemakers kind of preview event.</p>
<p>I chose my post&#8217;s title from the fact that Big Art Group uses technology in a way that makes me think of the <a href="http://www.thewoostergroup.org/">Wooster Group</a>, but their work is even more about mediating reality.  You can see the actors working on stage, doing what you watch on the three screens, but you&#8217;re supposed to watch just the screens, sort of.  In a world where New Yorkers used TV to confirm what was happening before their eyes on 9/11, it&#8217;s an approach that makes sense.  I referred to the Glamericans, with whom I marched on <a href="/2003/02/dissent-is-hot.html">February 15, 2003</a>, because some of the them are involved in the production, including Machine, who did the costumes.</p>
<p>Like Wooster Group, and many other interesting theatre groups, their works are &#8220;works in progress&#8221; basically forever.  When I asked a question about one of the more baffling characters in the talkback afterward (moderated by the lovely Mike Albo), I was told, &#8220;she&#8217;ll make more sense on Thursday.&#8221;  As in a lot of theatre that interests me, I was confused by the disjointed aspect of the work for the first third or so, but I was really into it, and the mystery of the plot, by the end.</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve been known to go to Wooster Group productions more than once during a run to watch how things evolve.  I suspect Big Art Group warrants a similar approach.</p>
