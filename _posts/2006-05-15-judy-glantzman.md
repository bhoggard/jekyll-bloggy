<p>We visited <a href="http://www.artcal.net/event/view/1/2185">this show</a> a couple of weeks ago, but I just now had time to write about it.  We are lucky to have two small drawings by her.</p>
<p>I found it interesting that the press release talks about Bosch and Picabia.  When I saw this painting, particularly the faces at the bottom, I thought of the use of skulls as necklaces in the Tibetan work I&#8217;ve seen recently at the <a href="http://www.rmanyc.org/">Rubin Museum of Art</a>.</p>
<p><img alt="rma-Chakrasamvara.jpg" src="/mt/archives/rma-Chakrasamvara.jpg" width="414" height="338" /></p>
<p>Mandala (Center and Circumference)<br />
Chakrasamvara<br />
Central Tibet<br />
1600 &#8211; 1699<br />
54.61&#215;44.45cm (21.50&#215;17.50in)<br />
Ground mineral pigment on cotton</p>
<p>This is a detail.  I don&#8217;t know if the link will work for every browser, but here is the <a href="http://www.rmanyc.org/Collections/65020z.cfm">page</a> for it.</p>
<p>The <em>New York Times</em> <a href="http://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9C0DE7DD113AF936A15754C0A9629C8B63&amp;sec=travel&amp;pagewanted=print">article</a> on the opening of the museum explains the symbolism of the skulls:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><span class="caps">SKULLS</span> <span class="caps">AND</span> <span class="caps">SEVERED</span> <span class="caps">HEADS</span> &#8212; Lacking soil for graves or wood for funeral pyres, Tibetans practice sky burial: bodies are left on a charnel ground for vultures. Senior monks meditate there overnight, contemplating impermanence. Skull necklaces are tokens of struggle in the war with Mara, the demon king.</p>
</blockquote>
