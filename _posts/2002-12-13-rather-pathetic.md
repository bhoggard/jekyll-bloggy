<p>Hilary Clinton sees that the shifting sentiments of the public regarding <i>l&#8217;Affaire Lott</i> require her to modify her initial tepid comments a bit.  I sent her an email saying I thought Lott should be censured by the Senate, at a minimum.  I think he should be forced out, except that a damaged <span class="caps">GOP</span> is probably the safest thing for all of us.</p>
<p>Click <span class="caps">MORE</span> to see the December 11 and December 13 versions of her e-mail.  I didn&#8217;t send a new e-mail in the intervening period.  It appears two days later that she is shocked, simply shocked, to find racist behavior coming from Mr. Lott.  I guess she hasn&#8217;t paid attention for the <a href="http://talkingpointsmemo.com/dec0202.html#121202854pm">last 20 years</a>.</p>

<p>December 13, 2002</p>
<p>Dear Mr. _______:</p>
<p>I agree that what Senator Trent Lott said at Senator Strom Thurmond&#8217;s<br />
100th birthday party is an extremely serious issue that must be addressed,<br />
and I appreciate your taking the time to contact my office about it.</p>
<p>The ideas that were embraced in Strom Thurmond&#8217;s 1948 campaign were<br />
strongly rejected by the American people because those ideas were wrong.<br />
More than a generation ago, Americans of different races and creeds put<br />
their lives on the line so that equality, justice, and economic<br />
opportunity would be available to every American.  We are far better off<br />
today as a nation because of their struggle.  Leaders in Senator Lott&#8217;s<br />
position should be bringing the American people together by celebrating<br />
our nation&#8217;s progress, not tearing us apart by suggesting that<br />
segregationist policies of the past were or are somehow acceptable.</p>
<p>The public outrage over Senator Lott&#8217;s comments is justified.  His<br />
comments were offensive and divisive.  In the face of great challenges at<br />
home and abroad, Americans need to join together in celebration of and<br />
support for the values that distinguish us as a great nation. One of these<br />
values is the belief in the equality of all people, and the very idea of<br />
segregation strikes at the heart of that equality.  It is my hope that<br />
every American leader, regardless of political affiliation, will strongly<br />
condemn these comments, and make clear that there is no place in our<br />
society, either in public discourse or private dialogue, for such<br />
statements that seem to condone racist policies of the past.</p>
<p>Our nation long ago rejected segregation and it is shocking that the man<br />
who holds the most powerful position in the Senate has suggested in 1980,<br />
1984, and again last week, that it is an acceptable practice.  This raises<br />
fundamental questions about his fitness to be Majority Leader.  I would<br />
not vote for someone to lead my caucus who made those offensive remarks<br />
but the Republicans will need to decide if this is how they wish to be<br />
represented.</p>
<p>You can be sure that I will continue to work with my colleagues to fight <br />
ideas and policies that are divisive or seek to take America back to a<br />
time when equality of opportunity was not a guarantee.  Thank you again<br />
for sharing your views, and please keep in touch.</p>

this or any other subject, please use my web response form at<br />
http://clinton.senate.gov/email_form.html.  Because of the size of my<br />
constituency and the growing number who choose to communicate through<br />
e-mail, I have selected the web form as the fairest and most equitable<br />
system to respond to e-mail messages.  E-mails that are sent as a direct<br />
response to my outgoing e-mail fall outside the system and do not receive<br />
a response.</p>
