<p>On Monday night we saw two short opera by <a href="http://www.martinu.cz/main.html">Bohuslav Martinu</a> at <a href="http://www.henrystreetchamberopera.org/current/index.html">Henry Street Chamber Opera</a>.  We&#8217;ve seen all of their productions, and I can&#8217;t recommend them enough. They do smart, well designed and directed operas with young casts that actually act &#8212; opera as theatre, not the semi-staged concerts that pass for productions at the Met.  One was a Dada opera from 1928 Paris, which included things like a young girl who falls in loved with a hanged man.  The other, written in the more grim historical moment of 1935 Prague, was more of a fairy tale set in the  forest, and featured <a href="http://www.theatermania.com/news/tmnews/index.cfm?story=2824&cid=1">Kathleen Chalfant</a> as a rather arch narrator.  I would watch that woman read a phone book.  She has a voice that makes one realize what a trained voice really is, rather than the wimpy voices of actors that are only interested in TV or film.</p>
<p>On the way to the Lower East Side on the subway, we both noticed a very hot guy standing near us with a great speaking voice.  Once we saw him again in the lobby, I heard people talking to him and realized he was <a href="http://www.henrystreetchamberopera.org/past/photos/malheurs_dido.html">David Adam Moore</a> who was an incredibly sexy Aeneas in their production of Purcell&#8217;s <A HREF="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/B00004C8TE/qid=1038422131/sr=2-3/tristanmedial-20">Dido and Aeneas</A>, complete with leather pants, a couple of tattoos, and a pierced nipple and belly button.  Swoon.</p>
<p>We had a great dinner afterward, including a bottle of <a href="http://www.doalella.com/eng/index_eng.htm">Alella</a>, at <a href="http://www.newyorkmetro.com/pages/details/2485.htm"><span class="caps">AKA</span> Cafe</a>.  Since it was the Monday before Thanksgiving, it was really quiet.  The maitre d&#8217; reminded me of <a href="http://www.filmbug.com/people/u/james_urbaniak.html">James Urbaniak</a>, and the whole staff was attractive and smart.  A strange guy, after finishing his meal, told them they better do something about Monday nights, or they weren&#8217;t going to make it, since eventually every store on the block would be a restaurant.  He described himself as an &#8220;impresario&#8221; who owned a restaurant in South Beach.  It&#8217;s the first time I&#8217;ve heard a person actually use that word outside of a period film.</p>
<p>The <span class="caps">LES</span> certainly has changed.  I remember my friends in a <a href="http://www.targetmargin.org/">Target Margin</a> production at Nada (on Ludlow) telling me about a shootout over drug turfs one night during a performance.  I first went to the neighborhood in 1989, to go have dinner at <a href="http://www.newyorkmetro.com/pages/details/8625.htm">El Sombrero</a>.  I had just moved here from <a href="http://www.tamu.edu/">Texas</a>, and my friends and I were researching every decent Mexican restaurant in the city.  I lived in Chelsea, which wasn&#8217;t exactly prosperous then (&#8220;excuse me sir/madam, but can I get in my building as soon as you&#8217;re finished&#8221;), but going to the <span class="caps">LES</span> felt very &#8220;edgy&#8221;.</p>
