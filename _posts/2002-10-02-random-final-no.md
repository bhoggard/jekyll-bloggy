<ul><li>One of the rooms we visited in the <a href="http://www.schoenbrunn.at/">Schoenbrunn</a> in Vienna was the room used by Franz Josef II for his audiences.  Any citizen of the empire had the right to an audience with the emperor, even peasants from the provinces.  They were instructed to wear formal attire if they had it, or native costume if not.  The descriptions from the time talk about farmers and country people wearing their native costumes, and today the waiting room before the audience room has mannequins wearing traditional garb from the various regions:  Tirol, Carinthia, Galicia, etc.</li>
<li>Empress Elizabeth, known as Sisi, the wife of Franz Josef II, was fanatical about her weight.  She dieted and excersized for hours every day.  One of the rooms in the palace still has exercise rings hanging in one doorway.</li>
<li>One of the popular drinks at this time of year in Austria is Sturm &#8212; &#8220;young wine&#8221; &#8212; barely alcoholic grape juice.</li>
<li>When we visited the <a href="http://www.neue-pinakothek.org/explorer/altef.html">Alte Pinakothek</a> in Munich to see Breughel, etc., we bought t-shirts in the museum shop by <a href="http://www.brown.edu/Facilities/David_Winton_Bell_Gallery/scully.html">Sean Scully</a>, with the words &#8220;Starr/McCarthy: Two Great Americans&#8221;.</li>
<li>I forgot to mention this, and it was important.  The Melk exhibit on the town during the Nazi period which I <a href="/2002/09/melknazis-then.html">mentioned earlier</a> specifically mentioned homosexuals in the list of people sent to concentration camps.  A lot of exhibits only talk about Jews, and maybe political prisoners, failing to mention homosexuals or gypsies.</li>
<li>I highly recommend <a href="http://www.timeout.com/vienna/accom.xml?ar=3&cg=1">Hotel Austria</a>, where we stayed in Vienna.</li>
<li>When we visited the <a href="http://www.wieskirche.de/">Wieskirche</a>, there were areas of the church where people had left gifts of thanks, or votive offerings in hope of some holy intervention.  There was  card for a fireman named Tommy, lost on 9/11.  There was also a letter of thanks from a man who had prayed there for God to send him a man, and he was thanking the church for his happiness after finding a wonderful mate.  I guess the Church really is often better on a local level than at the archbishop-on-up level.</li>
<li>I felt bombarded by news about Iraq while we were there.  Every paper &#8212; German, Austrian, French, etc. &#8212; had Bush and Iraq on the front page every day except 9/11.</li>
</ul>
