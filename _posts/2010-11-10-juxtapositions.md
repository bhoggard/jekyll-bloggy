<p>Over breakfast I mentioned these two news items today, and <a href="http://www.jameswagner.com/">James</a> suggested the connection.</p>

<p><img alt="jack-levine-welcome-home.jpg" src="/2010/11/10/images/jack-levine-welcome-home.jpg" width="600" height="393" class="mt-image-none" /></p>
<p>Jack Levine, <em>Welcome Home</em>, 1946, via his obituary in the  <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2010/11/10/arts/10levine.html?pagewanted=all">New York Times</a></p>
<blockquote>
<p>It shows an armchair general being honored at an expensive restaurant, a wad of food in one cheek. On his right sits a bored socialite. Two decrepit businessmen in tuxedos make up the rest of the party. The central figure, Mr. Levine said, was &#8220;the big slob who is vice president of the Second National Bank and the president of the Chamber of Commerce, only now he&#8217;s been in the Army.&#8221;</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>When &#8220;Welcome Home&#8221; was included in an exhibition of American culture in Moscow in 1959, the chairman of the House Committee on Un-American Activities mounted a campaign to have it removed. President Dwight D. Eisenhower said, &#8220;It looks more like a lampoon than art, as far as I am concerned,&#8221; but refused to intervene.</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>The uproar made Mr. Levine a star. He later told an interviewer, &#8220;You get denounced by the president of the United States, you&#8217;ve hit the top.&#8221;</p>
</blockquote>
