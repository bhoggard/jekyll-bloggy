<p>I saw &#8220;Radiant Baby&#8221;, the new musical about <a href="http://www.haring.com/art/subway/index.html">Keith Haring</a>, at the <a href="http://www.publictheater.org/">Public Theater</a> tonight.  My title above comes from one of the less successful songs of the evening, but overall I think it worked.  I really don&#8217;t like conventional musical theater, with songs that sound either like pop songs or something from a 1950s Broadway musical.</p>
<p>It starts off slowly, but it becomes quite compelling once we reach the <a href="http://users.tellurian.net/garage2/index2.html">Paradise Garage</a>.   The scene begins with a diva (sung by Angela Robinson) holding a note for about 45 seconds before the house music begins.  The whole scene is a funny and hot tribute to a time that once was.  It&#8217;s also the first time the music is actually interesting.</p>
<p>Three children (actually sung by children) act as the chorus, and given the fact that Haring worked with children throughout his career, it&#8217;s my favorite aspect of the musical.  Other highlights: the incredibly talented cast; Aaron Lohr is  compelling and very hot as Keith&#8217;s lover Carlos, and Julee Cruise as Andy Warhol.</p>
<p>I hadn&#8217;t realized that Tseng Kwong Chi and Haring were so close.  Some of the iconic photographs of Keith&#8217;s work, like the body painting of <a href="http://www.haring.com/art/body/bill.htm">Bill T. Jones</a> or of <a href="http://www.thebody.com/visualaids/web_gallery/2002/ramoran/06.html">Grace Jones</a>, are by Kwong.  He is a major character in the musical.</p>
<p>The visual design is quite good, and I like the fact that when they talk about his work near the end of his life they end up with his Ignorance=Fear / Silence = Death <span class="caps">ACT</span> UP <a href="http://www.vkp.org/artworks/22.jpg">poster</a>.</p>
<p>I think it&#8217;s worth seeing, but because of the history and the people involved, rather than because it is a great achievement in theater.  I really felt that a musical about Keith Haring deserved music with more edge than this, except for certain fabulous scenes like the Paradise Garage, provided.</p>
