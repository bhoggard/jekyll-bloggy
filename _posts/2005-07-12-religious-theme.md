<p>What a silly country we live in.  Via <a href="http://www.nynewsday.com/news/nationworld/nation/orl-asecholy12071205jul12,0,7610416.story?coll=ny-leadnationalnews-headlines&amp;track=rss">Newsday</a> (it&#8217;s in Florida of course):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The Holy Land Experience may seem like just another theme park, with its $30 admission charge, $5 parking fee and souvenir shops.</p>
<p>But, ending a four-year legal battle, a judge has ruled that the religious-themed attraction deserves the same tax-exempt status given to churches and museums. The ruling spares its owner from paying a delinquent property-tax bill that would have climbed to more than $1 million by the end of the year.</p>
<p>&#8230;</p>
<p>From the start, The Holy Land Experience was controversial. Local rabbis greeted it with trepidation, given its parent company&#8217;s stated mission of converting Jews to Christianity.</p>
<p>&#8230;</p>
<p>In her ruling, Circuit Judge Cynthia MacKinnon sided with Zion&#8217;s Hope, pointing out that the nonprofit company produces and distributes biblical cassettes, videos, books and CDs; publishes a religious magazine; broadcasts a syndicated radio show and supports missionaries in Israel.</p>
<p>&#8220;The property appraiser has failed to direct the court&#8217;s attention to any evidence that Plaintiff is using The Holy Land Experience to make money or for some other purpose than evangelizing and worshipping,&#8221; MacKinnon wrote.</blockquote></p>
