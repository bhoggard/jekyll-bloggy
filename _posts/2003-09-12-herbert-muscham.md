<p>Herbert Muschamp, my favorite writer on architecture and public spaces, <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2003/09/11/arts/design/11KELL.html?pagewanted=all&position=">writes</a> in the September 11, 2003 NY Times about receiving this collage from Ellsworth Kelly:</p>
<p><img alt="kelly-wtc.jpg" src="/mt/archives/kelly-wtc.jpg" width="500" height="425" border="0" /></p>
<blockquote>&#8220;On October 19, 2001,&#8221; it began, &#8220;I wrote a letter to you (that I never sent) in response to an article in The New York Times which discussed the controversy of what was to be planned for the `Ground Zero&#8217; space, asking artists and others for their opinions.&#8221; Mr. Kelly noted that two other artists, Joel Shapiro and John Baldessari, had urged that no building be erected at the site and that the architect Tadao Ando had made a similar proposition.<br />
<br />
&#8220;At that time, my idea for the World Trade Center site was a large green mound of grass,&#8221; he continued.<br />
<br />
More recently he saw an aerial photograph of the site on the cover of the Aug. 31 Arts &amp; Leisure section of The Times. &#8220;I was excited to see the site from this vantage point,&#8221; he wrote. &#8220;I was inspired to make a collage of my idea for the space of which I am sending you.&#8221;<br />
<br />
&#8220;I feel strongly,&#8221; he continued, &#8220;that what is needed is a &#8216;visual experience,&#8217; not additional buildings, a museum, a list of names or proposals for a freedom monument.&#8221; These, he said, are &#8220;distractions from a spiritual vision for the site: a vision for the future.&#8221;</blockquote>
<p>Now Muschamp writes:</p>
<blockquote>Some think that minimal art is our country&#8217;s most important contribution to culture. I&#8217;m not sure that this is true, or that it&#8217;s even an American invention. Mies van der Rohe had developed it in architecture before setting foot on our soil. Yet it is true that minimalism has held a particular appeal to Americans. It enables us to withdraw from the midst of complexity without denying the manifold reality of contemporary life.</blockquote>
