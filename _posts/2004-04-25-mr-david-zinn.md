<p>Our good friend David Zinn, costume and set designer extraordinaire, has a new <a href="http://www.mrdavidzinn.com/">web site</a>.  We first saw his work as a little baby designer with <a href="http://www.targetmargin.org/">Target Margin</a>, with whom he continues to work, in addition to more famous venues such as New York City Opera and Santa Fe Opera.</p>
<p>When we saw his <a href="http://www.mrdavidzinn.com/gallery/flavio/flavio.html">Flavio</a> at New York City Opera, the audience applauded the set changes.</p>
<p><i>Tamerlano</i> is an opera by Handel loosely based on the life of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamerlane">Timur</a>, a conqueror and ruler in 14th century Central Asia.  Suitably for an opera set in that region of the world, being produced last summer at an American music festival, David didn&#8217;t ignore what was <a href="/2003/04/barbarians.html">going on</a> at the time.</p>
<blockquote>In Handel&#8217;s rarely mounted Tamerlano, the defeated Bajazet&#8230;wore traditional sultan&#8217;s robes, while his corporate conquerors, barking orders and gleefully dividing the spoils, sported snazzy business suits. Mixing sexual and power politics, the libretto is nearly incomprehensible, but its centre&#8212;Bajazet losing his culture, his dignity and his daughter&#8212;is tragically clear. David Zinn&#8217;s cunning set was littered with ancient books and other looted treasures at the front of the stage, and endless, empty bookshelves at the rear.</blockquote>
&#8212; Jack Sullivan, <i>Opera Magazine</i>
